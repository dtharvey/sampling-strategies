Suppose you have a mixture with two types of particles: those with and those without an analyte of interest, where the fraction of particles with analyte is \(p\). If we sample a set of \(n\) particles from this mixture, then the number of particles with analyte follows a binomial distribution with an expected mean, \(\mu\), an expected standard deviation, \(\sigma\), and an expected relative standard deviation, \(\sigma_{rel}\) of

\[\mu = np \quad \quad \quad \sigma = \sqrt{np(1 - p)} \quad \quad \quad \sigma_{rel} = \sqrt{\frac{1 - p}{np}}\]

Exercise 1. The figure on the right shows 50 experiments in which \(n\) particles are drawn from a binomial distribution where \(p\) is the fraction of particles with analyte. The scale on the y-axis reports results as a percentage so the scale is independent of \(n\). Calculate the expected values for \(\mu\), \(\sigma\), and \(\sigma_{rel}\) if \(n = 10\) and \(p = 0.50\). How do the experimental results shown in the plot compare to the expected results?

Exercise 2. Leave the sample size at \(n = 10\) and adjust \(p\) from 0.01 to 0.99. Describe how \(p\) affects the results. Adjust \(p\) to 0.50 and adjust the sample size from \(n = 10\) to \(n = 1000\). Describe how \(n\) affects the results.

Exercise 3. Suppose you have a material to sample where \(p = 0.05\). How many particles, \(n\), do you need to collect to achieve a relative standard deviation of \(\sigma_{rel} = 0.01\)?
