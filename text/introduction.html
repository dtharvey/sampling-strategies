The goal of a quantitative analysis is to report an analyte’s concentration with both accuracy and with precision. To evaluate accuracy we consider systematic errors, such as failing to calibrate an instrument, and to evaluate precision we consider random errors, such as the noise in an instrument’s signal. Often overlooked are errors associated with collecting and processing samples. The photos on the right help to illustrate the importance of sampling. The upper photo shows a sample of the acid-base indicator erythrosin B (or EB, for short). Because EB is unstable in aqueous solutions—which makes it impossible to prepare and store in solution form—it is dispensed by combining it with NaCl and shaking to coat the salt crystals. The pile of EB-coated NaCl in the upper photo appears homogeneous, and, as seen in the lower photo, it appears reasonably homogeneous even when we zoom in on the pile.

To analyze the bulk material in the jar for EB we collect samples, bring them into solution, and measure their absorbance with a spectrophotometer. Although the analysis is accurate—the experimental mean for 32 samples is 0.118 %w/w EB compared to a known value of 0.116 %w/w EB—the precision of the analysis—a 95% confidence interval of \(\pm 15 \%\)—is surprisingly large. A careful analysis of the bulk sample allows us to partition the overall variance into the variance due to the lack of homogeneity between individual samples, the variance associated with preparing samples for analysis, and the variance associated with their analysis

\[\sigma_\text{overall}^2 = \sigma_\text{sampling}^2 + \sigma_\text{preparing}^2 + \sigma_\text{analyzing}^2\]

Despite the reagent’s appearance of homogeneity, as much as 99% of the overall variance is due to the lack of homogeneity at the level of individual samples; that is, the process of collecting samples is the weakest link in this analysis even though the material looks homogeneous. In this learning module we consider how we can collect and process samples to improve accuracy and precision.
